Visual Hierarchy

Green Chef

greenchef.com

Green Chef's website is a great example of visual hierarchy. Immediately, it draws your eye to the delicious meal that is on display at the center of the page. After taking in the goods, we then notice the headline, followed by the button which is our call to action, then the other descriptors, and finally the menu with the logo and another call to action at the top of the screen. If we take it all in, we can infer that Green Chef is a meal kit subscription service which offers a variety of meal plans for people with all types of dietary needs.

Fitt's Law

Nintendo

nintendo.com

Essentially, Fitt's law state's that the time required to move to a target is determined by the ratio between the distance to the target and the size of the target. The larger an object is, the closer it is. In terms of a website, larger elements are closer to the user than smaller ones; therefore, it makes sense that you would want to make the largest elements the ones that are used most often. On Nintendo's site we see Fitt's law in action. While there are several elements on screen, the ones that are used more often, the purchase and search buttons, are set apart from the rest by their greater size and bolder color.

PARC: Proximity

NCH Software

nchsoftware.com

Proximity is a way of demonstrating how the mind naturally categorizes objects based upon their distance from each other. When objects are close together, we assume that they are part of the same group. When they are far apart, we do not associate them with each other. We see the principle of proximity on the NCH Software website. At first, it almost appears cluttered; however, the number of items being displayed on such a small screen shows how efficient this layout is at delineating the various groups of the site. It is remarkable how much is on display while still preserving the white space around each group - a great use of proximity.
